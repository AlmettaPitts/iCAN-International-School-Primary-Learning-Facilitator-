Stop One  ·  Why Phnom Penh, Why iCAN

Small schools, big skies, and the kind of learning that lets children become themselves.

I have spent the last fifteen years moving between classrooms, counseling rooms, and community organizations on three continents — and the through-line has always been the same. I make spaces where young people feel safe enough to be curious. I came up in EPIK middle-school classrooms in South Korea, then in Korean universities, then in a Seattle clinical practice, then back to the graduate lectern at the University of Washington. What draws me to iCAN is the same thing that drew me to Sinju Middle School in Busan thirteen years ago: a school that is small enough to know every child's name, and ambitious enough to want those children to leave as creative, confident, globally-minded humans.

The International Primary Curriculum is a framework I already think in. Thematic, cross-curricular, internationally-minded, knowledge plus skills plus understanding — that is how I designed SEL-integrated English co-teaching in Korea, how I built "Creo en tu Mente" for multilingual learners in Federal Way, and how I teach my graduate students at UW today. Adaptable, creative, passionate, wellbeing-first is not a list of qualifications for me. It is the posture I already teach from.

And Cambodia, specifically Phnom Penh, is the right next step. I am a third-culture kid with deep practice in the rhythms of Southeast Asia — in partnering with local teachers, in building real relationships with families, in being a long-term faculty member instead of a transient one. I am arriving in August 2026 ready to stay, to contribute, and to give a whole contract-length of steady, unhurried care.

Stop Four  ·  In the Classroom, on a Tuesday

What creative, wellbeing-first teaching actually looks like when the door closes.

Because abstract words like "creative" and "adaptable" only matter if they translate into the small daily practices that make a child want to come back tomorrow. These are mine.

🌄

Morning arrival is a regulation practice

Children do not arrive ready to learn. They arrive carrying whatever the car ride, the breakfast, or the older sibling left with them. I start mornings with a consistent, low-stimulation ritual — a song, a breath, a welcome — so every child crosses a clear threshold into the classroom as a learner.

— because the nervous system comes first
🎨

Themes, not worksheets

IPC-style thematic units are my natural language. When we study "Water," we read about it, move like it, paint it, measure it, sing about it, and ask where it comes from in Phnom Penh specifically. Knowledge, skills, and understanding arrive together because that is how children's brains already work.

— cross-curricular by instinct
🗣️

Multilingual learners are the default, not the exception

From EPIK to Federal Way to UW, I have almost never taught a classroom where everyone shared one first language. I plan for visual supports, pre-teaching, partner talk, and sentence frames as standard practice — not as accommodation.

— so every child has a way in
📖

Story is the most honest teaching tool

Picture books, read-alouds, children's own stories on the wall. Story is how small people make sense of big feelings — and it is how I teach everything from phonics to conflict resolution to the difference between fair and equal.

— from puppets to paragraphs
🧭

Differentiation is the plan, not the adjustment

I plan three entry points into every core activity — a concrete version, a representational version, and an abstract version — so MP1 and MP3 learners of different readinesses can all join the same conversation. MTSS thinking, translated into small-classroom practice.

— same sky, different windows
🤝

Families are co-teachers

I write home often, in plain language, in both good-news and heads-up directions. A child's parent or grandparent is the most important adult in that child's education — my job is to make that partnership real, not ceremonial.

— especially in an international community
🧘🏽

Restorative, not punitive

When something hard happens — a shove, a mean word, a meltdown — I slow down instead of speeding up. Repair conversations, co-regulation, natural consequences. Children learn the most about being good people from how the adult responds when they are not.

— hard moments are the teaching
🌗

Endings matter as much as beginnings

Every day has a closing circle. Every unit has a celebration of learning. Every term has a transition ritual. Children feel safe when they know how something will end — and international children, who live inside a lot of goodbyes, need this more, not less.

— for globally mobile learners
✨

Joy is a teaching standard

If a lesson is not at least a little bit fun, I have not finished planning it yet. Dance breaks, silly voices, outdoor detours, the right picture book at the right moment. Joy is not a break from learning. Joy is the carrier wave.

— and children can tell when an adult is having fun too
Stop Five  ·  Safeguarding, in Plain Speech

When a school says "rigorous checks and staff conduct," I lean in — not back.

Because I have sat in the other chair. I have been the clinician documenting disclosures, the mandated reporter making the call, the adult who had to get this right. I know what this part of the job weighs. I welcome the weight.

Active clinical license

LICSW (Washington State, #SWI.LW.70098332). 4,000+ supervised clinical hours. Continuing education and professional ethics aren't just something I maintain — they are the terms of my professional existence.

Mandated-reporter fluency

I know how to notice, document, and escalate appropriately. I know what to say to a child in the moment and what not to say. I know the difference between a disclosure and a suspicion and I treat both with care.

Background checks and authentication

Federal FBI background check authentication in process. Apostille-ready MSW, BA, TEFL, and LICSW credentials. Documentation trail is clean, verifiable, and international-ready.

Safeguarding-trained

Formal safeguarding and child-protection training. Trauma-informed care certification. Experience applying these frameworks to K–12, university, and international school-adjacent contexts.

Staff-conduct culture

I work inside clear professional boundaries and model them for younger colleagues. I welcome conduct conversations in an interview. A school that takes this seriously is a school where children — and staff — are actually safe.

Culturally responsive safety

Safeguarding looks different across cultural contexts. I have practiced in U.S. K–12, Korean schools, and U.S. clinical settings. I know how to hold a universal standard of child safety while respecting local family and cultural realities.

A child's safety at iCAN will never come second to any lesson plan, any relationship, any professional convenience. If something is wrong, I will say so, through the right channels, at the right time. That is the ground the rest of my teaching stands on.

Stop Six  ·  Because Staff Wellbeing Is a School Culture Issue

I play hard too — and I think that's a teaching credential.

Burnt-out teachers cannot offer wellbeing-first learning. I protect my own life outside of the classroom so that I can bring a full, warm, unclenched human to my learners every morning. Here is what fills me up on the other side of the school gate.

🐾

Long walks with my pup

My 10-pound Mauzer is coming to Cambodia with me. Morning walks in a new city are how I fall in love with a place — and how I notice the small details a child might also notice.

our favorite thing is a quiet street at golden hour
💃🏽

Music and dancing as emotional regulation

Not metaphorically. Actually. Dance is how I come back into my body after a hard day. It is also the reason my classrooms have more movement than most.

afrobeat · soul · whatever has a great bassline
📖

Decolonial theory & literature

I read the heavy stuff for fun — especially writers from the places where I teach and the places I'm learning from. It keeps my practice honest and my curiosity wide.

currently: rereading bell hooks on pedagogy
🎬

Pop culture as therapeutic tool

I write worksheets that use The Summer I Turned Pretty, Mormon Wives, and country songs as entry points into CBT/DBT concepts. Pop culture is not beneath the classroom — it is the classroom door many kids walk through.

the culture meets you where you already are
🗺️

Traveling alone in new cities

I am a third-culture kid who has made her home in Illinois, Seattle, Busan, and Cheonan. Arriving somewhere unfamiliar and making it mine is a skill — and a joy — that I hope to keep practicing in Phnom Penh and beyond.

the best classrooms I've taught in were cities first
🌙

Global storytelling & The Other Life Method

I have a creative practice outside of teaching — storytelling, content, small projects that live under my Attemla Consulting work. A teacher with her own creative life makes more interesting things available to her learners.

my learners deserve an adult who is still learning

I remember being the first in my family to navigate something. I make myself available to younger professionals and students trying to do the same, and I will do this at iCAN too — quietly, on the side of my main job.

passing the ladder down

The food, the markets, the real street

I eat where the grandmothers eat. I learn kitchen words before academic words. I cannot wait to be a regular somewhere in Phnom Penh — and to tell my students about the place that knows my order.
